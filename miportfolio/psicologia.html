What You Don’t See

Visual campaign about social media and mental health

This project is a graphic campaign that reflects on the impact of social media on eating disorders, self-esteem, and body image pressure, especially among young people. Through two conceptual posters, it offers a visual critique of how social platforms shape the idea of the “perfect image.”

The first poster, with the message “The perfect image doesn’t feed you — it consumes you,” shows a dinner plate with a smartphone full of social media icons, likes, and idealized body images. It acts as a direct metaphor: online content becomes a toxic diet that doesn’t nourish, but causes anxiety and obsession.

The second poster, titled “What you don’t see on social media weighs more,” features an unbalanced scale. On one side, bright and appealing elements like likes and notifications; on the other, the hidden consequences: weight obsession, medication, extreme diets, and emotional pain. In the center, a girl in black and white, sitting in a defeated pose, represents the real cost of that constant pressure.

Both visuals share the same background, typography, and style, using digital collage as a creative tool. The campaign aims to expose what’s often hidden behind the screen, encourage critical thinking, and promote a more empathetic, conscious approach to image, identity, and mental health.
